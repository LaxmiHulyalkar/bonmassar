One area I have found particularly thought-provoking in the course of this work is the ongoing debate around whether B1+rms or SAR should serve as the primary safety parameter for MRI evaluation of passive implants. The current ASTM F2182 framework relies on temperature rise correlated to incident SAR, yet emerging literature and regulatory discussions suggest that B1+rms may offer a more reproducible, scanner-independent metric — particularly for patient-specific assessments where local field distribution matters more than whole-body energy absorption. I am genuinely curious to understand how this question is being approached in the context of patient-specific computational models, and whether a shift in baseline parameter could open more defensible pathways for in vivo safety assessment.

03 — The Research Gap
What ASTM F2182
cannot answer
ASTM F2182 explicitly states its method characterizes the “heating propensity of an implant” — not the prediction of heating in a specific patient during an actual MR procedure. I encountered this limitation directly while building simulation capability for real passive implants going into real adolescent patients.

Every simulation I ran was validated against a homogeneous gel phantom — a rectangular box with averaged tissue properties that bears little resemblance to the thoracic anatomy of a 14-year-old with surgically placed metallic hardware. No regulatory standard currently exists for patient-specific in vivo MRI safety simulation of passive metallic implants.

The consequence is clinical: MRI safety labels for these patients are based on conservative phantom estimates. This can result in MRI access restrictions that may not reflect actual patient risk — denying diagnostic imaging to patients who may actually be safe to scan.

Your lab’s work on patient-specific anatomical reconstruction, tissue-realistic electromagnetic modeling, and computational co-simulation in real human geometry is precisely the scientific infrastructure needed to begin addressing this gap.

01
No In Vivo Standard for Passive Implants
ASTM F2182 governs phantom-based testing only. No FDA-recognized framework exists for patient-specific in vivo RF heating simulation of passive metallic implants.
02
Patient Anatomy Ignored by Design
Tissue geometry, electrical properties, implant trajectory, and patient size vary significantly — particularly in pediatric and adolescent populations. Phantom models systematically ignore this variability.
03
Conservative Labels Restrict Imaging Access
Overly conservative phantom-derived MRI safety labels may unnecessarily restrict diagnostic imaging access for patients with passive implants — a clinically significant problem I observed firsthand.
